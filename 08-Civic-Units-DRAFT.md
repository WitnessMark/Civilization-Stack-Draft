## CU-01-Design-Philosophy-and-System-Notes

*Source: `CU-01-Design-Philosophy-and-System-Notes.md`*

CC-00 — Design Philosophy and System Notes v0.01

\---

Purpose

Capture the reasoning, constraints, and guiding principles behind the
system.

\---

I. CORE OBJECTIVE

CP1 — Primary Goal

Reduce daily life friction while preventing structural failure over time.

The system is not designed for:

* luxury optimization as status
* aesthetic novelty for its own sake

It is designed for:

* functional dignity
* long-term stability
* reduced burden
* maintainable structure

\---

II. DESIGN METHOD

CP2 — Failure-First Method

The system was not derived from:

* style
* ideology
* precedent copying

It was derived from:

* identifying recurring failures
* eliminating them structurally
* preventing their return through constraints

\---

III. PRIMARY INSIGHT

CP3 — Modern Failure Pattern

Modern systems fail because:

* infrastructure is hidden and inaccessible
* space is designed for vehicles, not humans
* buildings are optimized for first cost, not longevity
* daily life functions are fragmented across individuals

\---

IV. KEY PRINCIPLES

CP4 — Eliminate Known Failures

The system must prohibit:

* long corridors
* inaccessible utilities
* visible waste accumulation
* service deserts
* hierarchy-producing geometry

CP5 — Separate Systems From Living Space

Infrastructure exists, but does not intrude into daily life.

CP6 — Preserve Human Scale

The environment must support:

* immediate outdoor access
* short walking distances
* frequent spatial variation
* legible but non-sterile movement

CP7 — Remove Hierarchy By Design

The system must prevent:

* premium units
* privileged locations
* status conversion into structural advantage

CP8 — Design For Maintenance First

Everything important must be:

* accessible
* inspectable
* repairable without demolition

\---

V. SPATIAL PHILOSOPHY

CP9 — Experiential Goal

The environment should feel:

* legible but not obvious
* structured but not rigid
* varied but not chaotic
* calm without sterility

\---

VI. HISTORICAL REFERENCES

CP10 — Pre-Car Lessons

Valuable precedents include:

* nodes over corridors
* layered thresholds
* visible life with hidden systems
* walkable scale
* mixed living and making

\---

VII. NATURAL SYSTEM INFLUENCE

CP11 — Natural Logic

Natural systems should inform:

* adjacency
* distribution
* redundancy
* growth logic

Natural forms should not be copied literally when they reduce
buildability, maintainability, or clarity.

\---

VIII. STRESS TESTS APPLIED

CP12 — Test Categories

The system has been evaluated against:

* corridor confinement
* waste accumulation
* infrastructure failure
* service distance
* hierarchy formation
* environmental viability
* maintenance burden
* logistics friction

\---

IX. ANTI-PATTERN METHOD

CP13 — Constraint Method

Instead of asking:

* what is best?

The system asks:

* what must not be allowed?

This reduces complexity and prevents drift.

\---

X. SYSTEM STRUCTURE

CP14 — Layered Structure

The system is:

* cellular
* polycentric
* expandable
* non-linear

It is organized as:

* unit
* building
* cluster
* district
* node

\---

XI. LONG-TERM GOAL

CP15 — End State

The goal is an environment where:

* maintenance is ordinary
* failure is visible early
* daily life requires less coordination
* the built system resists degradation into dysfunction

\---

XII. SUMMARY

CP16 — Final Characterization

This system is not:

* a single building
* a style
* a utopia

It is:

* a constraint system
* a design grammar
* an operations-supportive environment
* a framework that makes bad environments difficult to build

\---

END OF CC-00 — DESIGN PHILOSOPHY AND SYSTEM NOTES v0.01

## CU-02-Structural-Specification

*Source: `CU-02-Structural-Specification.md`*

CC-A-01-Structural_Specification_v0.01

---

CC-A — STRUCTURAL SPECIFICATION v0.01

Purpose

Define all slow-changing, high-cost, and difficult-to-reverse physical systems governing land, layout, buildings, materials, infrastructure, and distribution.

This layer is pre-binding and governs all physical development.

---

I. LAND SYSTEM

CA1 — Land Integrity Requirement

Land must:

* support contiguous walkable settlement
* avoid permanent severance (highways, major rail, restricted corridors)

Fragmentation requiring dependency on external transit is prohibited.

---

CA2 — Water Priority

Each site must provide:

* at least two independent water sources or supply paths
* long-term viability projection ≥50 years

No site is valid without redundancy.

---

CA3 — Environmental Baseline (ERL)

All land must be classified using Environmental Readiness Levels (ERL):

* ERL-1 minimum for initial occupancy
* ERL-3 minimum for internal food production
* ERL-4 minimum for long-term agriculture

Design must assume worst-case initial ERL.

---

CA4 — Expansion Envelope

Each site must define:

* core zone
* expansion zone
* ecological buffer

Expansion must not require disruption of core systems.

---

II. SETTLEMENT GEOMETRY

CA5 — Walkable Radius

Maximum core diameter:

* ≤1.2 miles (20 minutes walking)

Preferred:

* ≤0.75 miles (12–15 minutes)

---

CA6 — Block System

Settlement must use perimeter blocks with internal commons.

Block dimensions:

* 60–120 meters per side

Rectangular or orthogonal geometries are required for:

* modularity
* repeatability
* infrastructure efficiency

---

CA7 — Distributed Function Rule

No critical system may be single-point centralized.

Applies to:

* food
* water
* sanitation
* childcare

---

CA8 — Green Ratio

Land allocation:

* 60–75% green
* 25–40% built

---

CA9 — Green Function Requirement

Green space must be functional:

* food production
* ecological restoration
* water management
* recreation

Decorative-only allocation is prohibited.

Green systems must form a continuous network, not isolated patches.

---

CA10 — Perimeter Logistics Rule

All heavy logistics terminate at the settlement boundary.

Allowed internally:

* pedestrian movement
* restricted service circulation

Prohibited internally:

* ordinary private vehicle circulation
* internal parking systems
* road geometries supporting general car use

Internal geometry must not support:

* turning radii for private vehicles
* sustained vehicle speeds
* continuous vehicle routing

Distribution corridors must not be convertible into general-purpose vehicle routes.

---

III. BUILDING SYSTEM

CA11 — Building Height Constraint

Allowed:

* 3 to 8 stories

Prohibited:

* single-story sprawl structures
* high-rise dependency systems

---

CA11A — Structural Dignity Requirement

All buildings and shared spaces must:

* avoid institutional or carceral appearance
* provide natural light, proportion, and material quality
* support psychological well-being

Design must not replicate:

* prison architecture
* degraded mass-housing typologies

---

CA12 — Structural Simplicity

Buildings must:

* use repeatable modular systems
* allow partial disassembly
* avoid non-repairable complexity

---

CA13 — Mixed-Use Requirement

All buildings must integrate:

* residential
* service
* shared/common functions

Single-use structures are prohibited.

---

CA13A — Vertical Functional Layering

Buildings must follow functional layering:

* ground level: shared / active / service functions
* mid-level: residential
* upper levels: quiet / private / low-traffic

---

CA14 — Internal Service Nodes

Each block must contain:

* hygiene/laundry access
* childcare access
* shared commons

---

CA15 — Circulation Design

Requirements:

* stairs must support full functionality
* elevators may not be required for baseline access
* corridors minimized

---

CA16 — Private Space Requirement

Each resident must have:

* physically separable private space
* secure storage
* closable boundary

---

IV. INFRASTRUCTURE SYSTEM

CA17 — Utility Redundancy

All systems must include:

* primary supply
* backup supply
* local storage

Applies to:

* water
* power
* waste

---

CA18 — Modular Infrastructure

All infrastructure must be:

* accessible
* repairable in segments
* replaceable without system collapse

---

CA19 — Surface Priority

Deep underground dependency is prohibited.

Preferred:

* surface or shallow-access systems

---

CA20 — Transport Structure

System must be:

* pedestrian-first
* vehicle-independent for daily life

Allowed:

* perimeter logistics
* emergency access

---

V. MATERIAL SYSTEM

CA21 — Material Classification Requirement

All materials must be classified:

* Prohibited (P)
* Restricted Transitional (RT)
* Boundary Transitional (BT)
* Preferred Permanent (PP)

Unclassified materials are prohibited.

---

CA22 — Prohibited Materials

Prohibited in structural or general use:

* persistent non-recoverable plastics (where alternatives exist)
* formaldehyde-heavy composites (low-grade press-board)
* PFAS-containing materials
* broadcast chemical dependency systems
* materials without end-of-life pathway

---

CA23 — Restricted Transitional Materials

Allowed only if:

* no viable alternative exists
* containment defined
* expiration defined
* removal pathway defined

---

CA24 — Boundary Transitional Materials

Allowed only at perimeter:

* heavy fuels
* industrial solvents
* large-scale mechanical systems

Never allowed in civilian core.

---

CA25 — Preferred Materials

Priority order:

1. reclaimed materials
2. renewable materials
3. fully recyclable materials

---

CA26 — Material Lifecycle Requirement

All materials must have:

* known source
* known lifespan
* defined reuse or disposal pathway

Unknown lifecycle = prohibited.

---

CA27 — Chemical Use Constraint

No system may depend on:

* broadcast chemical suppression (e.g., herbicides)

Preferred:

* mechanical
* biological
* ecological control

---

VI. DISTRIBUTION ARCHITECTURE

CA28 — Internal Distribution System

Settlement must include:

* perimeter intake zones
* internal distribution corridors
* decentralized service nodes

Internal distribution must support continuous flow of:

* food
* materials
* waste

without requiring resident logistical involvement.

---

CA29 — Service Radius Constraint

Core services must be reachable within:

* 250m: water/sanitation
* 300m: hygiene/laundry
* 500m: food/childcare
* 800m: learning

---

VII. PET AND ANIMAL SYSTEM

CA30 — Pet Integration Requirement

Animal presence must not degrade:

* sanitation
* air quality
* shared space usability

---

CA31 — Animal Waste Handling

All animal waste must have:

* immediate containment
* defined processing pathway
* no accumulation in shared space

---

CA32 — Resource Load Control

Animal population must not exceed system capacity.

---

VIII. BUILD SEQUENCING

CA33 — Permanent-First Rule

Build order:

1. utilities
2. food systems
3. sanitation
4. housing
5. enrichment

---

CA34 — No Throwaway Construction

Temporary structures must:

* convert to permanent use
  OR
* be fully removable

---

IX. STRUCTURAL PRIORITY RULE

CA35 — Irreversibility Priority

Systems that are:

* difficult to change
* high-cost to modify
* physically embedded

must be:

* over-specified
* constrained before construction

---

CA36 — Structural Override Protection

Civilian convenience may not override structural rules.

Any exception requires formal structural review.

---

END OF CC-A — STRUCTURAL SPECIFICATION v0.02

## CU-03-Building-Segment

*Source: `CU-03-Building-Segment.md`*

CC-A-02 — Building Segment v0.01

\---

Purpose

Define a repeatable residential building segment that:

* preserves human-scale living
* eliminates known failure patterns
* supports shared systems efficiently
* remains maintainable over long time horizons

\---

I. SEGMENT OVERVIEW

CA2-1 — Type

Low-rise, row-based residential base with integrated shared upper levels
and accessible service infrastructure.

CA2-2 — Height

Allowed:

* 3 to 4 stories total

CA2-3 — Segment Length

Maximum uninterrupted residential run:

* 120 to 200 ft

CA2-4 — Population Per Segment

Typical:

* approximately 40 to 80 residents

\---

II. VERTICAL STACK

CA2-5 — Level 1: Residential

Ground level must contain:

* row-house units
* direct outdoor access
* no corridor dependency

CA2-6 — Service Band

Between Level 1 and Level 2, the building must include a continuous
service band supporting:

* water
* waste
* electrical
* data
* ventilation

Residents do not access this layer.

CA2-7 — Level 2: Shared Active Uses

Level 2 should contain:

* childcare
* workshops
* community rooms
* light service functions

CA2-8 — Level 3: Shared Quiet Uses

Level 3 should contain:

* classrooms
* study rooms
* low-noise shared functions

CA2-9 — Optional Level 4

Where used, uppermost levels may contain:

* compact apartments
* elderly / disabled housing
* additional quiet or civic functions

\---

III. GROUND LEVEL RESIDENTIAL LAYER

CA2-10 — Unit Count

Typical segment capacity:

* 8 to 12 units per side, depending on width and break conditions

CA2-11 — Unit Size

Baseline target:

* 600 to 800 sq ft minimum efficient range

Expanded practical range:

* approximately 1,050 to 1,250 sq ft where higher comfort is desired

CA2-12 — Typical Proportions

Width:

* 18 to 24 ft

Depth:

* 30 to 45 ft

Long narrow railroad layouts are discouraged.

CA2-13 — Unit Requirements

Each unit must include:

* direct exterior-facing front door
* immediate access to shared outdoor space
* small threshold zone
* minimal kitchen or kitchen area
* flexible interior layout
* wet functions adjacent to service spine

CA2-14 — Outdoor Access Rule

All residential units must provide immediate, direct access to open-air
space without passing through shared interior circulation.

\---

IV. SEGMENT BREAKS

CA2-15 — Break Requirement

No continuous residential run may exceed approximately 150 to 250 ft
without:

* service / access core
* small node or court
* circulation break

CA2-16 — Service Core

Required break elements may include:

* vertical circulation
* utility access connection
* maintenance entry

CA2-17 — Node / Court

Preferred break elements may include:

* small shared green
* seating
* plants
* micro social space

\---

V. END CAPS

CA2-18 — Non-Residential End Caps

End caps must not be residential.

End caps serve:

* vertical access points
* service entry points
* logistics interface
* small shared functions

CA2-19 — Anti-Hierarchy Rule

End positions must not be used for residential units, to prevent spatial
hierarchy.

\---

VI. UPPER FLOOR ACCESS

CA2-20 — Access Separation

Public and shared upper-level uses must be accessible without requiring
passage through private residential units.

CA2-21 — Vertical Access

Buildings must use:

* end-cap stairs and elevators
* center service / access core where needed

Elevator redundancy must be sufficient to prevent single-point failure for
upper-floor residents.

\---

VII. SERVICE INFRASTRUCTURE

CA2-22 — Accessible Infrastructure

All critical infrastructure must be accessible without demolition and
without entering private living space.

CA2-23 — Vertical Service Cores

Vertical service cores must be located at:

* end caps
* mid-segment breaks where needed

CA2-24 — Shared Service Spine

Utilities should be consolidated into shared service spines serving
adjacent units, minimizing duplication while maintaining full access.

\---

VIII. CIRCULATION

CA2-25 — Prohibited Types

Prohibited:

* long enclosed corridors
* double-loaded hallway systems for primary residential access

CA2-26 — Required Types

Required:

* outdoor or semi-open movement paths
* multiple access points
* connection to node network

\---

IX. MATERIALS AND STRUCTURE

CA2-27 — Required Structural Characteristics

Buildings must be:

* durable
* repairable
* acoustically stable
* non-disposable

CA2-28 — Structural Logic

Buildings must use:

* simple load paths
* repetitive structural bays
* no unnecessary complexity

\---

X. ENVIRONMENTAL REQUIREMENTS

CA2-29 — Daylight

All residential units must receive daylight.

CA2-30 — Ventilation

Shared spaces must be ventilated and must not create stagnant air zones.

CA2-31 — Plant Viability

All planted areas associated with the segment must be environmentally
viable.

\---

XI. SUMMARY RULE

CA2-32 — Segment Intent

The building segment must:

* eliminate corridor dependence
* maintain human-scale living
* separate infrastructure from life
* prevent spatial hierarchy
* support modular expansion

\---

END OF CC-A-02 — BUILDING SEGMENT v0.01

## CU-05-Workforce-and-Operations

CC-B-01 — Workforce and Operations v0.02

\---

Purpose

Define the operational workforce structure and systems required to run
the community as an integrated civic organism rather than a fragmented
market of individually sourced services.

\---

I. CORE MODEL

CBW1 — Operations Principle

The city operates as a structured service organism, not a marketplace.

Work is:

* classified
* trained
* assigned
* monitored
* supported

Residents are not expected to individually source fragmented services.

CBW2 — Domain Classification

All work must be classified within defined operational domains with clear
training and progression pathways.

\---

II. OPERATIONAL DOMAINS

CBW3 — Infrastructure Domain

Includes:

* water
* energy
* sanitation
* utilities
* maintenance access systems

CBW4 — Built Environment Domain

Includes:

* construction
* repair
* fabrication
* building maintenance
* structural upkeep

CBW5 — Food Systems Domain

Includes:

* agriculture
* greenhouse work
* processing
* kitchens
* food distribution

CBW6 — Health and Human Maintenance Domain

Includes:

* medical care
* dental
* physical support
* body maintenance
* wellness and recovery support

CBW7 — Learning and Development Domain

Includes:

* childcare
* education
* training
* developmental support

CBW8 — Logistics and Distribution Domain

Includes:

* intake
* internal delivery
* storage
* routing
* materials movement

CBW9 — Creative and Investigative Domain

Includes:

* design
* engineering
* research
* crafting
* systems improvement
* material experimentation

CBW10 — Civic Operations Domain

Includes:

* scheduling
* planning
* audit
* monitoring
* records
* internal coordination

\---

III. ROLE STRUCTURE

CBW11 — Tiered Role Model

Each domain must include:

* entry roles
* skilled roles
* specialist roles
* oversight roles

CBW12 — Training Pathways

All roles must have:

* clear training requirements
* progression standards
* backup staffing

CBW13 — Redundancy Requirement

Critical functions must never depend on a single individual.
All roles require redundancy and trained backups.

\---

IV. ASSIGNMENT MODEL

CBW14 — Structured Assignment

Work is assigned through domain structure, not through market search,
gig work, or fragmented contracting.

CBW15 — Cross-Training

Cross-training is permitted where it improves redundancy, adaptability,
and resilience.

CBW16 — Replaceability

No role may become irreplaceable. Role design must support continuity
during absence, turnover, or reassignment.

\---

V. SERVICE INTEGRATION

CBW17 — Internal Service Replacement

Services that are currently fragmented in ordinary society should be
integrated into system operations where feasible.

Examples include:

* food preparation
* laundry
* childcare
* maintenance
* logistics
* consultation support

CBW18 — Role Redirection

Existing real-world skills may be redirected into system-relevant
functions.

Examples:

* auto mechanic → equipment / agricultural machine operator
* retail worker → logistics / distribution
* accountant → audit / resource tracking
* service seller → system support role

\---

VI. WORKLOAD DISTRIBUTION

CBW19 — Reduced Burden Goal

Since many burdens are removed from individuals, the total required
labor per person should decrease relative to fragmented systems.

CBW20 — Specialization Requirement

The system must preserve competence and specialization.
It may not assume that everyone can do everything adequately.

\---

VII. OPERATIONS GOVERNANCE

CBW21 — Outcome-Based Evaluation

All roles, assignments, and systems must be continuously evaluated for
effectiveness and adjusted based on measurable outcomes.

CBW22 — Operations Over Personality

Operations must rely on:

* rules
* documented procedures
* domain structures
* measured outputs

Not:

* charisma
* informal influence
* personality-based authority

\---

VIII. WORKFORCE SCALE ESTIMATE

CBW23 — Preliminary Workforce Estimate

A mature 100,000-person node may require an internal workforce in the
approximate range of 35,000 to 40,000 active roles across all domains,
including:

* direct service
* maintenance
* production
* education
* health
* logistics
* reserve / training pools

This estimate is structural and subject to refinement.

\---

IX. FAILURE CONDITIONS

CBW24 — Operational Failure

Failure occurs if:

* critical roles lack redundancy
* services depend on informal labor markets
* assignment becomes arbitrary or opaque
* system continuity depends on overwork
* workforce structure fragments into externalized dependency

\---

X. STRUCTURAL RULE

CBW25 — Workforce As Infrastructure

Workforce design must be treated as infrastructure.
The system may not assume that operations will emerge informally after
the physical environment is built.

\---

* consistently available

* routine cleaning services

* ongoing maintenance

\---

* locally available
* reliable
* safe

* scheduling complexity
* administrative tasks

* assist individuals
* reduce conflict
* optimize flow

\---

VII. SHARED SPACE AND GATHERING

* shared gathering areas
* quiet spaces
* flexible-use environments

\---

* purchasing

\---

* tools
* equipment
* appliances

through shared systems.

* varied schedules
* non-uniform work patterns

\---

* rest

## CU-09-Internal-Justice-and-Dispute-Framework

IJ-01-Internal\_Justice\_and\_Dispute\_Framework\_v0.01

\---

IJ-01 --- INTERNAL JUSTICE AND DISPUTE FRAMEWORK v0.01

Purpose

Define how disputes, misconduct, and system-protection issues are
resolved within the system without creating an internal legal class,
adversarial excess, or power drift.

Justice is treated as a bounded maintenance function, not a prestige
domain.

\---

I. CORE PRINCIPLE

IJ1 --- Conduct Over Identity

All review, mediation, and enforcement must be based on:

* observable conduct
* documented system impact
* defined rule violation

No action may be based on: - identity - belief - affiliation -
attitude - personality style

\---

IJ2 --- Minimal Force Requirement

All intervention must use the least structural force necessary to:

* stop harm
* restore function
* preserve safety

Escalation beyond necessity is prohibited.

\---

IJ3 --- Non-Adversarial Preference

The system must prefer:

* direct resolution
* facilitated resolution
* restorative correction

Punitive or adversarial processes are allowed only when lower-force
methods fail or safety requires immediate intervention.

\---

II. SCOPE

IJ4 --- Covered Matters

This framework applies to:

* interpersonal disputes
* misuse of shared systems
* safety violations
* repeated non-participation affecting system function
* property damage within system space
* harassment, intimidation, coercion, or disruption
* violations of shared access or boundary rules

\---

IJ5 --- Excluded Matters

This framework does not regulate:

* lawful private beliefs
* identity
* non-harmful lifestyle differences
* non-participation in optional cultural or social activity

Only conduct with system relevance may be reviewed.

\---

III. RESOLUTION TIERS

IJ6 --- Tier 1: Direct Resolution

Where safe and reasonable, individuals must be able to resolve disputes
directly.

Direct resolution may include: - conversation - clarification -
immediate correction

No formal record is required unless repetition occurs.

\---

IJ7 --- Tier 2: Facilitated Resolution

If direct resolution fails or is not appropriate, a neutral facilitator
may assist.

Facilitation must: - remain limited to the issue - avoid role
inflation - produce a clear outcome or next step

A brief record must be created.

\---

IJ8 --- Tier 3: Structured Review

If the issue affects safety, repeated conduct, system function, or
unresolved conflict, it moves to structured review.

Structured review must: - use a small rotating panel - review conduct
and documented impact - issue a bounded decision - produce a written
record

Panel membership must not be permanent.

\---

IJ9 --- Tier 4: System Protection Action

If a person presents immediate danger, repeated severe disruption, or
refusal of all lower-force remedies, the system may impose protective
action.

Protective action may include: - temporary restriction from defined
spaces - reassignment - supervised access conditions - temporary removal
from certain functions - removal from residency as a last resort

All Tier 4 actions require written justification and review.

\---

IV. PROCESS REQUIREMENTS

IJ10 --- Notice Requirement

Any person subject to structured review or protection action must
receive:

* notice of the conduct at issue
* the rule or boundary involved
* the review path being used

Secret accusations or undefined allegations are prohibited.

\---

IJ11 --- Response Opportunity

Any person subject to Tier 3 or Tier 4 review must have an opportunity
to:

* respond
* clarify facts
* present relevant context

The process must remain bounded and time-limited.

\---

IJ12 --- Decision Specificity

All formal decisions must state:

* what conduct occurred
* what system impact was found
* what action is required
* when the action ends or is reviewed

Undefined or open-ended sanctions are prohibited.

\---

IJ13 --- No Status Weighting

No decision may be influenced by:

* role importance
* external prestige
* internal popularity
* wealth
* credentials unrelated to the issue

Baseline equality applies throughout the process.

\---

V. STRUCTURAL SAFEGUARDS

IJ14 --- No Permanent Legal Class

The system must not create:

* permanent judges
* permanent prosecutors
* permanent legal specialists with internal sovereign authority

Interpretation-heavy roles must rotate, split, or remain auditable.

\---

IJ15 --- Interpretation Constraint

Internal justice may regulate:

* conduct
* access misuse
* safety risk
* functional disruption

Internal justice may not regulate: - conformity - tone preference -
cultural style - ideological alignment

\---

IJ16 --- Record and Transparency Requirement

All Tier 3 and Tier 4 actions must be:

* logged
* reviewable
* anonymized for general system reporting where necessary

The system must be able to detect: - bias patterns - role capture -
escalation drift - repeated misuse of process

\---

IJ17 --- Restoration Preference

Where possible, outcomes should aim to:

* repair damage
* restore trust
* restore safe function
* reduce future recurrence

Punishment without functional purpose is discouraged.

\---

VI. SAFETY AND EMERGENCY CONDITIONS

IJ18 --- Immediate Harm Response

Where there is immediate risk of harm, temporary action may precede full
review.

Such action must: - be narrow - be logged immediately - be reviewed
promptly

Emergency action does not exempt later review.

\---

IJ19 --- Boundary Protection

Private space, bodily safety, child safety, and baseline access
boundaries receive priority protection.

Violations involving these areas may escalate more quickly.

\---

VII. EXIT AND REMOVAL

IJ20 --- Exit as Resolution

Where continued participation is not workable, exit remains a valid
resolution.

The system is not required to retain every person under all conditions.

\---

IJ21 --- Removal Constraint

Removal from the system may occur only when:

* conduct creates repeated or severe harm
* lower-force methods have failed or are clearly inadequate
* system safety or function cannot otherwise be preserved

Removal may not be used for: - disagreement - nonconformity - lawful
belief - prestige conflict

\---

VIII. FAILURE CONDITIONS

IJ22 --- Justice System Failure

Failure occurs if:

* the process becomes status-driven
* bias patterns emerge without correction
* temporary actions become indefinite
* undefined allegations are used
* the justice function becomes a power center

\---

IX. STRUCTURAL RULE

IJ23 --- Justice as Maintenance

The internal justice system exists to maintain safety, boundaries, and
function with minimal force and minimal drift.

It may not become: - an ideology engine - a prestige domain - a
sovereign institution inside the system

\---

END OF IJ-01 --- INTERNAL JUSTICE AND DISPUTE FRAMEWORK v0.01

A brief record must be created.

\---

IJ8 --- Tier 3: Structured Review

If the issue affects safety, repeated conduct, system function, or
unresolved conflict, it moves to structured review.

Structured review must: - use a small rotating panel - review conduct
and documented impact - issue a bounded decision - produce a written
record

Panel membership must not be permanent.

\---

IJ9 --- Tier 4: System Protection Action

If a person presents immediate danger, repeated severe disruption, or
refusal of all lower-force remedies, the system may impose protective
action.

Protective action may include: - temporary restriction from defined
spaces - reassignment - supervised access conditions - temporary removal
from certain functions - removal from residency as a last resort

All Tier 4 actions require written justification and review.

\---

IV. PROCESS REQUIREMENTS

IJ10 --- Notice Requirement

Any person subject to structured review or protection action must
receive:

* notice of the conduct at issue
* the rule or boundary involved
* the review path being used

Secret accusations or undefined allegations are prohibited.

\---

IJ11 --- Response Opportunity

Any person subject to Tier 3 or Tier 4 review must have an opportunity
to:

* respond
* clarify facts
* present relevant context

The process must remain bounded and time-limited.

\---

IJ12 --- Decision Specificity

All formal decisions must state:

* what conduct occurred
* what system impact was found
* what action is required
* when the action ends or is reviewed

Undefined or open-ended sanctions are prohibited.

\---

IJ13 --- No Status Weighting

No decision may be influenced by:

* role importance
* external prestige
* internal popularity
* wealth
* credentials unrelated to the issue

Baseline equality applies throughout the process.

\---

V. STRUCTURAL SAFEGUARDS

IJ14 --- No Permanent Legal Class

The system must not create:

* permanent judges
* permanent prosecutors
* permanent legal specialists with internal sovereign authority

Interpretation-heavy roles must rotate, split, or remain auditable.

\---

IJ15 --- Interpretation Constraint

Internal justice may regulate:

* conduct
* access misuse
* safety risk
* functional disruption

Internal justice may not regulate: - conformity - tone preference -
cultural style - ideological alignment

\---

IJ16 --- Record and Transparency Requirement

All Tier 3 and Tier 4 actions must be:

* logged
* reviewable
* anonymized for general system reporting where necessary

The system must be able to detect: - bias patterns - role capture -
escalation drift - repeated misuse of process

\---

IJ17 --- Restoration Preference

Where possible, outcomes should aim to:

* repair damage
* restore trust
* restore safe function
* reduce future recurrence

Punishment without functional purpose is discouraged.

\---

VI. SAFETY AND EMERGENCY CONDITIONS

IJ18 --- Immediate Harm Response

Where there is immediate risk of harm, temporary action may precede full
review.

Such action must: - be narrow - be logged immediately - be reviewed
promptly

Emergency action does not exempt later review.

\---

IJ19 --- Boundary Protection

Private space, bodily safety, child safety, and baseline access
boundaries receive priority protection.

Violations involving these areas may escalate more quickly.

\---

VII. EXIT AND REMOVAL

IJ20 --- Exit as Resolution

Where continued participation is not workable, exit remains a valid
resolution.

The system is not required to retain every person under all conditions.

\---

IJ21 --- Removal Constraint

Removal from the system may occur only when:

* conduct creates repeated or severe harm
* lower-force methods have failed or are clearly inadequate
* system safety or function cannot otherwise be preserved

Removal may not be used for: - disagreement - nonconformity - lawful
belief - prestige conflict

\---

VIII. FAILURE CONDITIONS

IJ22 --- Justice System Failure

Failure occurs if:

* the process becomes status-driven
* bias patterns emerge without correction
* temporary actions become indefinite
* undefined allegations are used
* the justice function becomes a power center

\---

IX. STRUCTURAL RULE

IJ23 --- Justice as Maintenance

The internal justice system exists to maintain safety, boundaries, and
function with minimal force and minimal drift.

It may not become: - an ideology engine - a prestige domain - a
sovereign institution inside the system
