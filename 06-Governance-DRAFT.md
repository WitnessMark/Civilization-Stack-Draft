# GOV-00 — -- Purpose

This framework defines the structural architecture of a government
designed to remain limited, correctable, and durable across
generations.  
The system ensures that authority is bounded, interruptible, reversible,
and subject to renewal.

Core inversion: **Authority does not continue automatically. Authority
must periodically justify its continuation.**

# GOV-01 — -- Sovereignty

Sovereignty resides in the living people across generations within
ecological limits.

Principles: - Government exists to serve people and preserve the
future. - No authority may permanently bind future generations. -
Ecological life-support systems form the boundary conditions of
sovereignty.

* Education
* Public health care access

# GOV-06 — -- Civic Burden Limits

Total Civic Burden (TCB) includes all mandatory financial and
administrative obligations.

Targets: - Recommended burden: 25--35% of disposable income - Hard upper
bound: 40%

Administrative compliance target: - 40--80 hours annually

If limits are exceeded: - Automatic simplification review - Redesign of
regulatory structure

## GOV-10-Failure-and-Rollback-Protocol

*Source: `GOV-10-Failure-and-Rollback-Protocol.md`*

# GOV-10 — -- Failure and Rollback Protocol

When institutional failure occurs:

1. Detect threshold breach
2. Activate stop mechanism
3. Conduct independent review
4. Roll back to last compliant state
5. Publish findings
6. Renew, redesign, or dissolve the institution

All institutions must maintain a documented rollback and replacement
pathway.

*Source: `GOV-11-Office-Selection-Competence-Requirements.md`*

# GOV-11 — -- Office Selection \& Competence Requirements

Public office requires both democratic legitimacy and demonstrated
competence.

Eligibility requirements for major offices: - Constitutional literacy -
Domain-relevant expertise - Administrative competence -
Conflict-of-interest transparency

Screening mechanisms: - Independent competence review panels - Public
disclosure of qualifications - Structured debate and scenario review

Removal conditions: - Corruption or capture - Demonstrated incapacity -
Structural rule violations - Renewal review failure

Offices may continue without fixed term limits as long as competence,
trust, and renewal conditions remain satisfied.

## GOV-12-Judiciary-Charter

*Source: `GOV-12-Judiciary-Charter.md`*

# GOV-12 — -- Judiciary Charter

The judiciary interprets law and resolves disputes while remaining
structurally limited.

Structure: 1. General Courts 2. Administrative Courts 3. Constitutional
Court

Core rules: - Courts may interpret but may not expand authority beyond
constitutional scope. - Interpretations that enlarge state authority
trigger automatic constitutional review. - All decisions must include
transparent reasoning tied to constitutional text.

Judges serve long but reviewable tenures and may be removed for
corruption, incapacity, or structural violation.

## GOV-13-Service-Ratio-Standards

*Source: `GOV-13-Service-Ratio-Standards.md`*

# GOV-13 — -- Service Ratio Standards

Essential public services must scale proportionally with population.

Examples of baseline service ratios: - Hospital capacity: minimum per
population threshold - Library access: defined geographic radius -
Emergency shelter capacity: disaster-ready ratio - Postal access:
universal service coverage - Education capacity: classroom and teacher
ratios

Ratios are periodically reviewed to ensure adequacy and efficiency.

The constitution mandates proportional service; statutes define current
ratios.

*Source: `GOV-20-Institutional-Dissolution-Replacement.md`*

# GOV-20 — -- Institutional Dissolution \& Replacement

Institutions must remain replaceable.

Dissolution triggers include: - structural corruption - persistent
authority drift - renewal failure - systemic harm threshold breach

Dissolution process: 1. structural investigation 2. temporary authority
freeze 3. rollback to last compliant operational state 4. restructuring
or replacement authorization

Replacement systems must preserve essential public services during
transition.

No institution may become permanently irreplaceable.

# Operational Government Framework --- Technical Appendices

## GOV-A1 --- Civic Burden Accounting Standard

Purpose: Provide a consistent method for measuring the Total Civic
Burden (TCB) placed on individuals.

Components of TCB: - Direct taxes (income, property, etc.) - Mandatory
fees and licenses - Compulsory insurance or contribution programs -
Regulatory compliance costs - Administrative time required for legal
compliance

Metrics: - Financial burden ratio (percentage of disposable income) -
Administrative burden (hours per year) - Structural burden distribution
across income levels

Thresholds: - Target burden: 25--35% - Maximum burden: 40% -
Administrative compliance: 40--80 hours annually

Governments must publish annual Civic Burden Statements for
transparency.

\---

## GOV-A2 --- Public Infrastructure Population Ratios

Purpose: Ensure that essential services scale proportionally with
population.

Examples of baseline ratios: - Hospitals: defined beds and staff per
population threshold - Schools: classroom and teacher ratios -
Libraries: geographic accessibility standards - Emergency shelters:
disaster capacity planning ratios - Postal access: universal service
coverage

Ratios must be periodically reviewed to maintain service quality and
accessibility.

\---

## GOV-A3 --- Monetary Issuance Rules

Purpose: Ensure currency serves as a stable medium of exchange rather
than a tool of manipulation.

Core principles: - Transparent issuance mechanisms - Publicly auditable
monetary policy - Separation between currency management and political
short‑term interests

Issuance constraints: - All currency creation recorded in public
monetary ledger - Inflation management mechanisms publicly documented -
Monetary authority subject to structural review cycles

\---

## GOV-A4 --- Data and Algorithm Governance

Purpose: Regulate government use of digital systems affecting rights and
governance.

Rules: - Personal data collection must be purpose‑bound - Data retention
must be time‑limited - Automated decision systems must be auditable -
Individuals must be able to challenge algorithmic decisions

Government algorithms affecting rights must be publicly documented and
reviewable.

\---

## GOV-A5 --- Constitutional Renewal Protocol

Purpose: Define periodic review of the constitutional system itself.

Renewal mechanisms: - Long‑interval constitutional review conventions -
Citizen‑initiated amendment proposals - Legislative supermajority
amendment pathway

Principle: The constitutional system must remain durable but never
permanently frozen.

Renewal cycles ensure the system can adapt while preserving core
structural protections.

## GOV-X1-Opt-In-Governance-Architecture

*Source: `GOV-X1-Opt-In-Governance-Architecture.md`*

Opt-In Governance Architecture
A governance system where authority only exists when actively accepted.
Power only applies when people actively accept it.
Authority must continually opt into existence.
Power should be temporary unless justified.
Authority should not exist by default - It must continually justify its existence.
Power should not become permanent.
Power must remain stoppable.

The simplest and most durable solution is:

Primary name:
Bounded Power

Formal document:
Structural Constitution for Bounded Power

System reference:
Bounded Power Architecture





Why the Project Moved Away From That Direction

The consent framing has a major structural weakness.

Governance often affects people who did not consent.

Examples:

environmental regulation

policing

infrastructure

taxation

emergency action

Because of that, a pure opt-in governance system cannot realistically operate.

So the architecture evolved to something more structurally robust:

Instead of:

People opt into governance

The system became:

Authority must continually opt into existence.

This is a brilliant inversion.

Instead of:

people must opt out of power

The system becomes:

power must opt into continuation

That inversion appears throughout your final architecture.

# GOV-14 — -- Monetary \& Financial System

Money exists to facilitate trade and stable economic coordination.

Currency governance principles: - Transparent issuance rules - Public
auditability - Independence from short-term political manipulation -
Protection against private capture

Prohibited practices: - Hidden monetary issuance - Systemic predatory
debt structures - Concentrated control over currency creation

Required practices: - Public monetary reporting - Transparent inflation
management - Clear separation between productive finance and speculative
manipulation

*Source: `01-Governance/GOV-15-Transition-Plan.md`*

# GOV-15 — -- Transition Plan

Transitioning from an existing system to this framework requires staged
implementation.

Phase 1 --- Stabilization - Preserve existing services and legal
continuity - Establish constitutional integrity institutions

Phase 2 --- Structural Alignment - Implement renewal mechanisms -
Establish civic burden accounting - Create citizen review bodies

Phase 3 --- Institutional Conversion - Reorganize executive
administration - Implement service ratio standards - Transition
financial governance

Phase 4 --- Full Operational Status - Renewal cycles active - Structural
constraints enforced - Citizen oversight mechanisms fully functional

The transition must prioritize stability, continuity of essential
services, and protection of rights.

# GOV-16 — -- Emergency Governance Protocol

Emergencies require rapid response but must remain structurally
temporary.

Emergency declaration requirements: - Defined triggering event -
Explicit scope of authority - Defined geographic and functional
boundaries

Emergency timeline: - Day 0: emergency declaration - Day 30: mandatory
legislative review - Day 60: supermajority renewal threshold - Day 90:
automatic expiration unless constitutional conversion occurs

Emergency powers may not: - Permanently expand institutional authority -
Suspend courts indefinitely - Override renewal mechanisms - Restrict
citizen review structures

Every emergency concludes with: - full operational audit - rollback to
pre-emergency authority levels - publication of findings
