## ECON-00-Purpose-and-Scope

*Source: `ECON-00-Purpose-and-Scope.md`*

# ECON-00 — Purpose & Scope
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Core block, module 00.*

This framework defines the structural architecture of an economy designed to remain harm-limited, bounded, competitive, and interruptible across generations.

**First principle — prevent and repair harm first; then act within that limit.** The economy's first obligation is not to produce, grow, or accumulate — it is to prevent harm, and to repair the harm it causes. Production, exchange, ownership, and growth are activities *permitted inside* that limit, never goals that override it. An economy is judged first by the harm it avoids and repairs, and only then by what it builds.

It is a *constraint system*, not an economic ideology — not "capitalist" or "socialist" by label. It specifies what an economy **must not be allowed to do**: cause harm it cannot repair, concentrate beyond the point where it can be stopped, monopolize the necessities, extract faster than it provides, or outgrow the living systems it depends on. Everything else is left open.

## Stated wagers

This domain rests on value choices. They are named plainly here so they can be argued with, rather than smuggled in as if they were neutral:

- **Harm-first ordering** — preventing and repairing harm outranks production and growth.
- **Bounded over maximal** — an economy that stays stoppable and correctable is worth more than one that grows fastest.
- **Essentials as a floor** — access to necessities sits *beneath* the market, not inside it.
- **Coordination over extraction** — money and finance exist to coordinate provision, not to pull value out of it.

These are defensible positions, not the only possible ones. They are the foundation, and a reader is meant to be able to see them and contest them.

## What ECON governs

The structure of economic life inside those limits: what the economy is for (ECON-01), money (02), markets (03), essential resources (04), ecological bounds (05), and renewal of economic authority (06); how it operates — ownership, labor, pricing, credit, taxation, and the provision floor (07 → 12); and the safeguards and measurable standards that hold it all in place (13 → 16, A1 → A5).

## What ECON does not govern (handoffs)

- **Who may issue and oversee money** → GOV. ECON defines money's economic role; GOV defines the authority.
- **Applying these rules to a specific built community** → NODE (Make/Buy/Sell, Financial/Phasing).
- **Diagnosing existing economic institutions** → BPF, whose records document the failures ECON is built to prevent.
- **Long-horizon and intergenerational economics** → LT.
- **Governance of algorithmic economic systems** → DATA and GOV's data/algorithm rules.

## Standing rules

Every ECON module operates under the domain's structural principles (see `ECON-MASTER`, §5): harm is prevented and repaired before activity proceeds; economic authority does not continue automatically; institutions stay interruptible, reversible, and auditable; access to essentials is a floor, not a market outcome; nothing becomes too big to fail, stop, or leave; natural limits are hard constraints; and money serves coordination, not extraction.

## ECON-01-What-the-Economy-Is-For

*Source: `ECON-01-What-the-Economy-Is-For.md`*

# ECON-01 — What the Economy Is For
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Core block, module 01.*

An economy exists to **provide for needs and coordinate effort** — to move resources, labor, and goods to where they are needed, among people who will never meet. That is its purpose. Everything else it does is either in service of that, or drift away from it.

**Provision.** The economy's first useful function is meeting real needs — food, shelter, care, tools, the means of a stable life — beginning with the essentials floor (ECON-12) and extending outward. An economy that produces enormous output while leaving real needs unmet is not succeeding; it is mis-aimed.

**Coordination.** Its second function is matching effort to need without central omniscience: dividing labor, signaling through prices what is scarce and what is plentiful, and letting strangers exchange and cooperate. Money, markets, and contracts are coordination *tools* — useful exactly to the degree they do this job, and no further.

**What it is not for.** The economy is not for extraction — pulling value out of people or places while returning less provision than it takes. It is not for accumulation as an end in itself, growth pursued for its own sake, or the conversion of economic position into power that escapes accountability. These are the recurring ways the purpose inverts: the tool of coordination quietly becomes an engine of concentration.

**The test.** An economic activity is legitimate to the degree that it provides or coordinates, and suspect to the degree that it merely extracts or concentrates. This is not a moral mood — it is the working measure the later modules apply: to ownership forms (ECON-07), to finance (10), to taxation (11), and to the anti-capture safeguards (13–14).

All of this runs inside the harm limit set in ECON-00. Provision and coordination are what the economy is *for*; not causing harm it cannot repair is what it must respect *while* doing it. Where the two collide, harm-first wins.

*Wager named: that an economy should be judged by how well it provides and coordinates — not by how much it produces or how fast it grows. That is a choice, and a contestable one.*

## ECON-02-Money-and-Currency

*Source: `ECON-02-Money-and-Currency.md`*

# ECON-02 — Money & Currency
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Core block, module 02.*

Money is **infrastructure, not merchandise.** It is a shared instrument that lets strangers coordinate — measuring value, settling exchange, and deferring it across time — so provision can flow without barter or central omniscience (ECON-01). Like a road or a water main, its worth is in what it carries, not in itself.

**What money is for.** Three working functions, in this framework's order of importance:

- **medium of exchange** — what lets a baker and a builder trade without a double coincidence of wants;
- **unit of account** — a common scale for comparing and planning;
- **store of value** — a way to hold purchasing power across time, but a *bounded* one, because this is the function that most easily inverts into extraction.

**Core constraint — money must stay a means.** Under the harm-first and coordination-over-extraction wagers, money must not be allowed to become:

- a primary engine of accumulation — value making more value while returning no provision (pure rent and financial extraction are held to ECON-10's limits);
- a route to unaccountable power — monetary concentration converted into control that escapes oversight (handed to the anti-capture safeguards, ECON-13–14);
- a lever against the essentials floor — no monetary mechanism may price people out of necessities (ECON-04, ECON-12);
- opaque — money's movement at systemic scale must remain observable and auditable (ECON-15).

**Stability is a harm question.** A currency must be stable enough to do its coordinating job. Severe inflation and deflation are not merely technical failures; they move wealth around and fall hardest on those with the least buffer — which makes monetary stability a harm-prevention requirement, not a growth preference. The measurable issuance and stability rules live in **ECON-A2**.

**Who issues it is not ECON's call.** This module defines money's economic *role and behavior*. The authority to create, back, and oversee currency — who may do it, under what mandate, with what limits — belongs to **GOV** (GOV-14, Monetary & Financial System; GOV-A3, Monetary Issuance Rules). ECON sets the behavioral requirements; GOV holds the lever. Where the two meet: GOV defines the issuer, ECON defines what the issued money must and must not do.

*Wager named: that money is best treated as public infrastructure — a coordination tool kept stable, legible, and bounded — rather than as a commodity whose accumulation is itself the goal. The opposite view, that capital accumulation is the proper engine of an economy, is coherent and widely held; this framework does not take it, and says so plainly.*

## ECON-03-Markets-and-Competition

*Source: `ECON-03-Markets-and-Competition.md`*

# ECON-03 — Markets & Competition
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Core block, module 03.*

A market is a **coordination tool, not a verdict.** It is good at one narrow, valuable thing: discovering — through many independent choices — what is needed and what is scarce, and signaling it through price so effort can move toward it (ECON-01). It does that job only as long as no single actor can dictate its terms.

**Competition is the operating condition, not a bonus.** A market coordinates honestly only when entry is open, information is reasonably available, and no participant is large enough to set prices or shut rivals out. Strip those away and the mechanism doesn't merely weaken — it inverts. Prices stop telling the truth, the strong set the terms for the weak, and the market becomes an instrument of extraction wearing the costume of exchange.

**The default drift is concentration.** Markets do not stay competitive on their own. Left unbounded, advantage compounds: winners buy or starve rivals, gatekeepers form, and a sector that began as many traders ends as a few price-setters. Treating this drift as an accident rather than the baseline is the central mistake this framework refuses to make. Competition is something a system *maintains*, against pressure — never something it can assume.

**Core constraint — a market is legitimate only while it stays competitive and non-captured.** Concretely:

- no actor may grow large enough to set terms unilaterally or exclude rivals (enforced by the anti-capture firewall, ECON-13, against the concentration thresholds in ECON-A1);
- essential sectors carry tighter limits — the market for necessities may not be captured at all (ECON-04);
- markets must stay open to entry and exit, and legible in their prices and terms (ECON-15);
- markets must carry their ecological costs rather than externalize them (ECON-05);
- where a market *cannot* be kept competitive — a natural monopoly such as a grid, a pipe network, a single rail line — it is not left to private capture; it moves under public oversight or direct provision instead.

**A market is not the right tool for everything.** This framework uses markets where they coordinate well, and bounds or replaces them where they fail. The essentials floor (ECON-12) sits *beneath* the market, never inside it; ecological commons and natural monopolies sit outside it. Reaching for a market by reflex, in places it cannot coordinate honestly, is itself a failure mode.

*Wager named: that markets are tools to be kept competitive by deliberate structural limits — not a self-correcting natural order that does best when left alone. The opposing view, that markets regulate themselves and intervention mostly does harm, is coherent and widely held; this framework does not take it. It holds that concentration is the default, and that an honest market is a maintained condition, not a found one.*

## ECON-04-Essential-Resources

*Source: `ECON-04-Essential-Resources.md`*

# ECON-04 — Essential Resources
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Core block, module 04.*

An **essential** is anything whose absence is not a hardship but an *injury*: water, food, shelter, warmth, basic health care, sanitation — and, at the outer edge, the ecological life-support the rest depend on (ECON-05). The defining test is harm: to be denied an essential is to be harmed, directly. That is what sets this category apart from everything else the economy handles.

**Core rule — access to essentials is a floor beneath the market, not a market outcome.** Markets ration by ability to pay. For ordinary goods that is acceptable coordination. For essentials it is not, because rationing survival by price means the people with the least are the ones who go without — harm landing exactly where the framework's first principle forbids it (ECON-00). So essentials are governed by a different rule than the rest of the economy: **a guaranteed floor of access, with the market permitted above it and never beneath it.**

Two protections carry this:

- **No monopolization.** No actor may corner an essential — a water supply, a staple crop, an energy source, a necessary medicine — and set its terms. The bar is stricter here than the general anti-capture rule (ECON-13): for essentials, concentration is suspect even when it stays nominally within price limits, because the stakes are survival, not preference.
- **A guaranteed floor.** Everyone has baseline access to essentials independent of market position. You may buy more or better above the floor; you may not be priced out of the floor itself. The operational machinery — how the floor is provided and funded — lives in **ECON-12 (Provision Floor)**; this module sets the principle it has to satisfy.

**The category is bounded, and stays bounded.** "Essential" must be defined explicitly and held narrow. If everything is essential, nothing is protected — inflating the category would hollow out the rule. The list is finite, public, and revisable, with ecological life-support (water, air, soil) as its outermost ring (ECON-05).

**Essentials and natural monopolies reinforce each other.** Many essentials arrive through natural monopolies — the water main, the grid, the one clinic in a region. Where an essential *and* a natural monopoly coincide (ECON-03), the case for public oversight or direct provision is at its strongest, and private capture at its least acceptable.

This is not redistribution offered as an ideology; it is harm-first applied to the things whose absence is itself the harm. The floor exists for the same reason the whole domain does.

*Wager named: that access to necessities is a floor beneath the market, not something the market should allocate — survival is not a market outcome. The opposing view, that markets allocate even essentials most efficiently and that guaranteed floors create dependency or distortion, is coherent and widely held; this framework does not take it, on harm-first grounds.*

## ECON-05-Ecological-Bounds

*Source: `ECON-05-Ecological-Bounds.md`*

# ECON-05 — Ecological Bounds
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Core block, module 05.*

An economy runs inside a living system it did not build and cannot replace. Air, water, soil, a stable climate, the web of living things — these are not inputs the economy manufactures; they are the conditions it depends on to exist at all. They set the outer wall, and the economy operates inside it or not at all.

**Hard constraints, not externalities.** The standard move is to treat ecological damage as an "externality" — a cost pushed onto the commons, the future, or someone downstream, and at most priced after the fact. This framework refuses that framing at the limit. Some ecological boundaries are *hard*: floors and ceilings that activity may not cross at any price, because crossing them causes harm that cannot be repaired — and irreparable harm is the bright line the whole domain is built around (ECON-00). You cannot buy your way past an irreversible threshold.

**Two tiers.** The bound separates what can be traded from what cannot:

- **Hard limits — not for sale.** Irreversible or catastrophic thresholds — species extinction, climate tipping points, the destruction of an aquifer or living soil, the accumulation of persistent toxins — are ceilings. No price clears them; no growth justifies them. This is the outer ring of the essentials rule (ECON-04): the life-support every other essential rests on.
- **Bounded costs — accounted, not ignored.** Reversible, sub-threshold ecological costs may be measured and internalized, so the activity that creates them carries their weight instead of offloading it. Pricing and trading are allowed here, as tools — but only *beneath* the hard limits, never as a way to reach past them. The measurable machinery is **ECON-A4 (Ecological & Externality Accounting Standard)**.

**Why this is harm-first, not a separate green agenda.** Degrading life-support is harm — at the largest scale, the least reversible, falling hardest on people who never consented and aren't born yet. Ecological bounds are simply harm-first applied at civilizational range. The long-horizon stewardship of these limits is held with **LT**; the governance-level mandate sits in **GOV-18 (Ecological Stewardship Code)**. ECON-05 is the economic constraint: activity stays inside the wall.

**This is not shrinkage as a goal.** Within the bounds, the economy is free — to produce, trade, and grow. The bound is a limit, not a target. But honesty requires naming the corollary: where current activity already exceeds what the living system can sustain, staying inside the wall means that activity must come down. The framework does not pretend otherwise.

*Wager named: that some ecological limits are hard walls, not prices — that you cannot pay your way past irreversible harm. The opposing view, that all costs including ecological ones can be efficiently priced and traded, and that markets handle the environment better than fixed limits do, is coherent and widely held; this framework does not take it, on harm-first and irreversibility grounds.*

## ECON-06-Renewal-Logic

*Source: `ECON-06-Renewal-Logic.md`*

# ECON-06 — Renewal Logic
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Core block, module 06.*

No economic authority is permanent. Every charter, every special privilege, every dominant position is granted for a term and must earn its renewal — or it lapses. Continuation is the thing that has to be justified; expiration is the default. This is the Stack's renewal principle (ECON-00) turned directly on economic power.

**Why default-to-expiration.** Economic power drifts toward entrenchment the same way markets drift toward concentration (ECON-03): once established, it works to make itself permanent and unaccountable. The usual defense is endless vigilance — fighting, after the fact, to dislodge a power that has already dug in. This framework chooses a structural defense instead: grant nothing as permanent, and the entrenched position never forms, because it was always going to expire unless actively renewed. You do not have to win a battle to remove what was never given for keeps.

**What must renew.** The principle reaches every form of standing economic power:

- charters and operating authority of large or systemic firms;
- any monopoly or near-monopoly position, even a permitted one;
- special privileges — subsidies, exclusive licenses, regulatory exemptions, public guarantees;
- and the mandates of the economic-oversight bodies themselves. The watchers renew too; there is no permanent regulator any more than a permanent firm.

**The renewal test.** To renew, an authority must show it still serves provision and coordination (ECON-01), still sits inside the harm limits and ecological bounds (ECON-00, ECON-05), has not concentrated past the thresholds (ECON-A1), and remains stoppable, reversible, and visible. Meeting the test renews the term; failing it lets the term end. The review machinery is **ECON-A5 (Structural Renewal Review Protocol)**.

**Lapse is not punishment.** A term ending for lack of renewal is the ordinary, scheduled default — not a penalty, not a finding of wrongdoing. Keeping it neutral is what stops renewal from becoming a political fight: nothing has to be proven *against* an authority to let it expire; it simply has to fail to prove itself *for* continuation. Removal *for cause* — emergency interruption, forced dissolution — is a separate path, and lives in **ECON-16**.

**Nothing is exempt, including this.** The renewal principle applies to itself. These rules, and the renewal protocol that enforces them, are themselves subject to review and renewal. A framework that exempts its own machinery from the limits it imposes on everyone else has already failed the first test it sets.

*Wager named: that economic authority should default to expiration and earn renewal again and again — permanence assumed by nothing. The opposing view, that durable, stable economic institutions create the certainty investment and long-range planning require, and that constant renewal breeds instability, is coherent and widely held; this framework does not take it, holding that entrenchment is the greater long-run danger.*

## ECON-07-Ownership-and-Enterprise

*Source: `ECON-07-Ownership-and-Enterprise.md`*

# ECON-07 — Ownership & Enterprise Forms
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Operational block, module 07.*

Ownership is a grant, not a fact of nature. The right to use a thing, to keep others off it, to profit by it and pass it on, is real and worth protecting — but it is something the system extends and bounds, not an absolute that stands before the system and dictates to it. Once that is clear, the rest of this module is just deciding where the bounds sit.

**Ownership is a bundle of rights, each conditional.** To own is to hold a set of rights — use, exclusion, profit, transfer — and each holds only inside the limits already set: it ends where it would cause irreparable harm (ECON-00), corner an essential (ECON-04), breach an ecological wall (ECON-05), or concentrate past the thresholds (ECON-A1). Ownership is protected up to those lines and not across them. Nothing here abolishes private property; it declines to make it absolute.

**Rights are strongest where power is smallest.** A sole proprietor and a systemic conglomerate are not governed by the same rules. As an enterprise grows in scale and systemic weight, more obligations attach — transparency (ECON-15), oversight (ECON-14), renewal (ECON-06), anti-capture (ECON-13). The reasoning is harm-first: small-scale ownership can do little damage and earns wide latitude; large-scale economic power is exactly where capture and irreparable harm concentrate, so it is held tightest. Ownership rights scale *inversely* with the power they carry.

**Enterprise forms are plural, chosen for fitness.** The framework mandates no single way to organize production. Sole proprietorships, partnerships, cooperatives, employee-owned firms, public enterprises, nonprofits, investor-owned corporations — all are permitted, chosen for fitness to the task rather than for ideology. The one standing requirement is that every form, at scale, stay stoppable, transparent, and non-capturing.

**The corporation is a charter, not a birthright.** The large limited-liability corporation exists by public grant — limited liability and indefinite life are privileges the public extends, not properties a firm holds by nature. Because they are granted, they are conditional and renewable (ECON-06): the privilege continues while the firm stays inside the limits, and lapses when it does not.

**Some things are not ownable.** No one may own what would let them hold others hostage — the essential chokepoints and natural monopolies route to public oversight (ECON-03, ECON-04), not private title. And people are never property; labor is a relationship, not a thing owned, and is governed in ECON-08.

*Wagers named: (1) that ownership is a conditional, bounded grant rather than a near-absolute right that precedes the system. The opposing view — that strong, near-absolute property rights are foundational to freedom and prosperity, and conditioning them is overreach — is coherent and widely held; this framework does not take it, on harm-first and anti-capture grounds. (2) That distributed forms — cooperative and employee ownership — may be favored where they fit, because they spread the power that ownership carries. This is a deliberate thumb on the scale, not a mandate — and one placed knowingly, because the scale was never neutral to begin with: existing arrangements already tilt toward concentrated ownership, so a counterweight is correction, not bias. The opposing view, that investor ownership allocates capital most efficiently and the system should favor no form at all, is real, and named.*

## ECON-08-Labor-and-Income

*Source: `ECON-08-Labor-and-Income.md`*

# ECON-08 — Labor & Income
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Operational block, module 08.*

Labor is work people do — never a thing they are sold as. A market may coordinate work; it may never purchase the person doing it. That line, drawn first, is what the rest of this module protects: the worker's safety, dignity, and freedom to leave are not among the terms up for negotiation.

**Work must not injure.** The first rule of labor is the first rule of everything here — harm comes before activity (ECON-00). Safety, non-coercion, and limits on exploitation are not perks granted when an employer can afford them; they are conditions work must meet to be permitted at all. No one may be made to trade their health or safety for their income.

**The floor is what makes labor free.** Put plainly: if you can always eat, stay housed, and get care no matter which job you hold — or whether you hold one at all — then you can turn down a bad one, and work you are free to walk away from is work, not a trap. That is the load-bearing move. Because essentials are guaranteed beneath the market (ECON-04, ECON-12), no one labors under the threat of losing food, shelter, or care, and that single fact turns a labor market from coercion into choice: a person who can survive without a given job can actually refuse it, and a market where refusal is real is the only kind that coordinates honestly instead of extracting quietly. Income from work sits *above* that secured floor; it is never the only thing between a person and ruin.

**Bargaining power is maintained, not assumed.** Employers, like all economic actors, drift toward concentrated power over those who depend on them (ECON-03). So the framework protects the structural conditions of fair bargaining — the right to organize, to information, to exit — as a standing counterweight, not a favor. Labor's footing against capital is something a system keeps level on purpose; left alone, it tilts.

**No arrangement may trap a worker.** Debt bondage, livelihood-barring non-competes, company-town lock-in — anything that removes a worker's ability to leave is void. Exit is the worker's form of reversibility (ECON-00), and it is not waivable.

**Labor and the surplus it creates.** The framework does not dictate a single split of what work produces, but its thumb toward distributed and employee ownership (ECON-07) reflects a plain view: those whose labor creates value should be able to share in it, not only draw a wage from it. The same instinct extends to automation — when machines do the work, the secured floor (ECON-12) means displacement need not mean destitution.

*Wagers named: (1) that survival must be decoupled from work — a guaranteed floor is what makes a labor market genuinely voluntary. The opposing view, that tying survival to work is necessary to keep people willing to work and that decoupling breeds dependency, is coherent and widely held; this framework does not take it, on harm-first and non-coercion grounds. (2) That labor needs a maintained counterweight to capital. The opposing view, that such protections reduce flexibility and distort the market, is real — and named here as the cost this framework knowingly accepts.*

## ECON-09-Pricing-Exchange-and-Trade

*Source: `ECON-09-Pricing-Exchange-and-Trade.md`*

# ECON-09 — Pricing, Exchange & Trade
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Operational block, module 09.*

A price is a signal, not a verdict. It carries one piece of information — how scarce a thing is against how much it is wanted — so that effort and resources can move toward where they are needed (ECON-01). It says nothing about what a person or a thing is *worth*. Read as a moral judgment, a price lies; read as a coordinating signal, it is one of the most useful tools an economy has.

**A price only coordinates when it forms honestly.** The signal works when prices arise from open competition with reasonable information (ECON-03). It breaks when prices are fixed, rigged, or faked — collusion, manipulation, and deceptive pricing don't bend the signal, they sever it, leaving a number that looks like information but carries none. Honest formation is the condition; the rest of this module is what protects it.

**A price must carry its full cost.** A price that leaves out the harm behind a thing — the polluted river, the exhausted soil, the offloaded risk — is not a low price. It is a lie with the bill mailed to someone else, usually the commons or the future (ECON-05). Within the accounted tier (ECON-A4), prices are made to carry the costs they create, so a buyer sees the true weight of what they are buying rather than a discount paid for by a stranger.

**Price does not ration survival.** For ordinary goods, letting price ration who gets what is acceptable coordination. For essentials it is not — the floor sits beneath the market, and no price stands between a person and a necessity (ECON-04). This bites hardest in scarcity: charging the desperate the most, when demand is inelastic because survival is at stake, is not clever allocation but extraction at the worst possible moment, and it is bounded.

**Exchange is voluntary and informed.** Trade binds only when both sides consent and neither is deceived; fraud and coercion void it. The same rules cross borders — trade between communities may not become a channel for exporting harm, dumping costs, or capturing another's essentials (international detail in ECON-17). Trading that provisions and coordinates is legitimate; trading built to corner, manipulate, or extract — above all in essentials — is held to the same test as everything else (ECON-01).

*Wager named: that prices must carry their full cost to count as honest, and that price-rationing stops at the essentials floor. The opposing view — that prices should reflect supply and demand alone, that even crisis pricing efficiently allocates scarce goods, and that mandating full-cost pricing distorts the signal — is coherent and widely held; this framework does not take it, on harm-first grounds.*

## ECON-10-Credit-Debt-and-Finance

*Source: `ECON-10-Credit-Debt-and-Finance.md`*

# ECON-10 — Credit, Debt & Finance
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Operational block, module 10.*

Finance is **coordination across time.** Where money moves provision between strangers (ECON-02), credit moves it between *now and later* — letting a thing be built today against the value it will produce tomorrow. A loan for a workshop, a bond for a water main: at its honest best, finance is how an economy acts before it has saved up. That is the function. Everything else it does is in service of it, or drift away from it.

**Core constraint — finance must finance provision, not extract from it.** The wager set in ECON-01 and ECON-02 bites hardest here: credit and capital are coordination tools, legitimate to the degree they move resources toward real production and need, and suspect to the degree they merely pull value out. Lending that builds the workshop coordinates; lending engineered so the interest, fees, and claims return more than the activity ever provides is extraction wearing finance's clothes. The test is the same one the whole domain runs.

**Debt must not become a trap.** A debt that can be serviced is a tool; a debt structured so it can never be cleared is a leash. The framework voids the financial equivalents of the labor traps in ECON-08 — debt that compounds past any hope of repayment, terms that strip the essentials floor (ECON-04, ECON-12), lending that targets the desperate precisely because their need makes them unable to refuse. Borrowing is permitted; engineered un-repayability is not.

**Systemic finance is held tightest.** Finance is where economic power concentrates fastest and most quietly — a position built of claims on others, often invisible until it fails. So large and systemic financial actors carry the heaviest obligations: transparency (ECON-15), oversight (ECON-14), renewal of their charters (ECON-06), and the anti-capture limits (ECON-13). A financial institution large enough that its failure would harm everyone is, for that reason, not allowed to operate unseen or unstoppable (ECON-16).

**The issuer is GOV's; the behavior is ECON's.** Who may create money and back the system belongs to GOV (GOV-14); ECON defines how credit and finance must behave once that system exists. Where they meet: GOV holds the lever, ECON sets what finance built on it may and may not do.

*Wager named: that finance is a coordination tool to be judged by the provision it enables, not an engine whose own returns are the point. The opposing view — that capital markets allocate best when free to maximize their own return, and that constraining them dampens growth and investment — is coherent and widely held; this framework does not take it, on harm-first and coordination-over-extraction grounds.*

## ECON-11-Taxation-and-Public-Revenue

*Source: `ECON-11-Taxation-and-Public-Revenue.md`*

# ECON-11 — Taxation & Public Revenue
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Operational block, module 11.*

Taxation is how the shared layer is paid for. The essentials floor, the commons, the oversight that keeps power bounded — none of it funds itself; taxation is the means by which an economy sets aside part of what it produces to sustain the things no private buyer will. Read as confiscation, it looks like loss. Read as the price of a floor everyone stands on, it is provision.

**Core function — fund the floor, and bound concentration.** Taxation in this framework does two jobs, and the second is as load-bearing as the first. It raises the revenue that funds guaranteed provision (ECON-12) and the public functions of the domain. And it is one of the structural tools that keeps economic power from concentrating past the point of accountability (ECON-13). A tax system is judged on both: does it fund the floor, and does it work with the limits rather than against them.

**Revenue serves provision first.** The first claim on public revenue is the essentials floor — the harm-first ordering of ECON-00 applied to the budget. Funding the floor is not one priority among many to be traded off against the rest; it is the thing the floor's guarantee (ECON-12) depends on being real. A revenue system that cannot reliably fund baseline provision has failed its first job regardless of what else it accomplishes.

**Tax is a lever on concentration, not only a till.** Because economic power drifts toward entrenchment (ECON-03, ECON-06), how an economy taxes shapes whether that drift compounds or is checked. The framework treats the structure of taxation — what it falls on, and how it bears on accumulated economic power — as a deliberate counterweight, not a neutral mechanism for collecting a fixed bill. This is a stated thumb on the scale, placed knowingly, for the same reason the ownership rules tilt toward distribution (ECON-07): the scale was never level to begin with.

**What it must not do.** Taxation may not breach the floor it funds — a revenue system that taxes people below the line into hardship has inverted its own purpose. Nor may it become a tool of unaccountable power in its own right; like every standing economic function, the bodies that levy and administer it are transparent (ECON-15) and renewable (ECON-06).

*Wager named: that taxation legitimately serves to bound concentration, not merely to raise a fixed sum at least cost. The opposing view — that tax should fund necessary functions as efficiently as possible and otherwise stay neutral toward the distribution of wealth — is coherent and widely held; this framework does not take it, on the grounds that concentration is the default drift and a counterweight is correction, not bias.*

## ECON-12-Provision-Floor

*Source: `ECON-12-Provision-Floor.md`*

# ECON-12 — Provision Floor
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Operational block, module 12.*

The provision floor is the essentials rule (ECON-04) made operational. ECON-04 sets the principle — access to necessities sits beneath the market, never inside it. This module is the machinery: what the floor guarantees, how it is provided, and why it holds without conditions. It is the single piece of architecture the rest of the domain leans on most, because it is what makes refusal real — of a bad job (ECON-08), a predatory loan (ECON-10), an extortionate price (ECON-09).

**Core rule — a guaranteed baseline of essentials, reaching everyone, independent of market position.** Below the floor, access does not depend on what you can pay, what you have earned, or whether you hold a job. Above it, the market runs as normal — buy more, buy better, no ceiling. The line is not a ration that replaces the market; it is a floor the market is never allowed beneath. The measurable content — what counts as adequate access to each essential — lives in **ECON-A3 (Provision Floor Standard)**.

**The floor is unconditional, and that is the point.** A floor you keep only while you behave, comply, or qualify is not a floor; it is a lever, and a lever held over someone in need is exactly the coercion the essentials rule exists to forbid. Conditioning survival on conduct re-creates the trap the floor was built to remove. So baseline access is not means-tested into a tool of control — it is guaranteed, because a guarantee with strings is not a guarantee.

**It is funded deliberately, not left to chance.** The floor costs real resources, and pretending otherwise is how floors quietly fail. Its funding is the first claim on public revenue (ECON-11), committed rather than discretionary — for the same reason infrastructure maintenance must be committed when a system is built: a guarantee funded only when convenient is not guaranteed. Where an essential arrives through a natural monopoly (ECON-03), provision may be public directly rather than purchased.

**Bounded, not boundless.** The floor covers essentials — the finite, public, harm-defined list of ECON-04 — and no more. It is not a guarantee of comfort, status, or every good; inflating it past necessities would hollow out the rule (ECON-04) and make it unfundable. The floor is narrow on purpose, so it can be absolute.

*Wager named: that baseline access to essentials must be unconditional and survival decoupled from market position — not earned, means-tested, or conditioned on conduct. The opposing view — that unconditional provision breeds dependency and that access should be tied to contribution or need-testing — is coherent and widely held; this framework does not take it, on harm-first and non-coercion grounds.*

## ECON-13-Anti-Capture-Firewall

*Source: `ECON-13-Anti-Capture-Firewall.md`*

# ECON-13 — Anti-Capture Firewall
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Safeguards block, module 13.*

This is the limit the rest of the domain keeps pointing at. Markets drift toward concentration (ECON-03), economic power toward entrenchment (ECON-06), and ownership toward absoluteness (ECON-07) — and every one of those modules names a firewall that holds the line. This is that firewall: the structural cap on how large and how dominant any single economic actor may become.

**Core rule — no actor may grow past the point where it can set terms unilaterally or can no longer be stopped.** The bar is not "is this actor behaving well." It is "could this actor, by virtue of its size and position, dictate terms to others or escape being checked." Concentration is treated as a hazard in itself, before any abuse occurs, because by the time the abuse arrives the power to prevent it is already gone. The measurable lines — market share, systemic weight, control of chokepoints — live in **ECON-A1 (Concentration Thresholds)**.

**Structural, not behavioral.** The standard regulatory move is to permit concentration and police conduct — let actors grow without limit, then punish the abuses. This framework refuses that order. Policing conduct fights the symptom and leaves the cause; an actor large enough to capture its market is one mistake or one merger away from doing so regardless of today's good behavior. The firewall caps the *power*, not the *conduct*, because structure is what holds when vigilance lapses.

**Essentials get the strictest line.** The general cap is strict; for essentials it is stricter still (ECON-04). Where survival is at stake, concentration is suspect even within nominal price limits, because the harm of capture lands exactly where the framework's first principle forbids it. And where a market cannot be kept competitive at all — a natural monopoly (ECON-03) — the answer is not a permitted monopolist but public oversight or direct provision.

**The firewall renews, and binds its own enforcers.** The thresholds and the bodies that enforce them are not permanent fixtures; they are subject to renewal (ECON-06) and transparency (ECON-15) like everything else. An anti-capture authority that itself becomes an unaccountable, permanent power has reproduced the disease it was built to prevent.

*Wager named: that concentration must be capped structurally, before abuse, rather than permitted and policed by conduct. The opposing view — that size alone is not harm, that large firms often serve consumers well, and that intervening on structure rather than proven conduct punishes success — is coherent and widely held; this framework does not take it, on the grounds that captured power cannot be reliably policed once it exists.*

## ECON-14-Systemic-Oversight

*Source: `ECON-14-Systemic-Oversight.md`*

# ECON-14 — Systemic Oversight
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Safeguards block, module 14.*

Some economic actors are too small to matter much when they fail, and some are large enough that their failure becomes everyone's. The firewall (ECON-13) caps how big an actor may get; this module governs what is required of the ones that, even within the cap, carry systemic weight. The principle is the inverse-scaling rule of ECON-07 made into a standing duty: the more an actor's failure or conduct would harm others, the more it must be watched.

**Core rule — systemic weight is a condition of heightened oversight, not a reward of escaping it.** An actor whose collapse, withdrawal, or misconduct would ripple across the economy operates under obligations a corner enterprise does not: continuous visibility into its real position (ECON-15), oversight of its systemic risks, and a credible plan for being wound down. Scale does not earn a firm freedom from scrutiny; it earns the opposite, because scale is exactly where the harm concentrates.

**Nothing becomes too big to fail.** The phrase names a specific failure: an actor so woven into the economy that letting it collapse is unthinkable, so it is propped up no matter what it does. That is capture by another name — the privatized gain, socialized risk that the coordination-over-extraction wager (ECON-00) forbids. The framework's answer is structural: a systemic actor must be kept *resolvable* — able to fail or be wound down without taking the economy with it — which means the question "what happens when this fails" is answered before it does, not during the emergency.

**Oversight is visibility plus a working off-switch.** Watching is not enough on its own; oversight that can see a problem but cannot act on it is theater. So systemic oversight pairs with the interruption and dissolution mechanisms of ECON-16: the capacity to actually stop or unwind an actor for cause. Seeing and stopping are one function split across two modules — this one establishes the watching, ECON-16 the acting.

**The watchers are watched.** Oversight bodies are themselves systemic actors, and the rule turns on them too: their mandates renew (ECON-06), their operations are transparent (ECON-15), and they hold no permanent, unaccountable authority. A regulator that cannot itself be checked has become the concentration it polices.

*Wager named: that systemic scale obligates heightened oversight and built-in resolvability, accepted as a cost of operating at that scale. The opposing view — that such oversight burdens the productive firms an economy depends on and that markets discipline even large actors adequately — is coherent and widely held; this framework does not take it, on the grounds that "too big to fail" is capture, and capture cannot be left to self-correct.*

## ECON-15-Transparency-and-Visibility

*Source: `ECON-15-Transparency-and-Visibility.md`*

# ECON-15 — Transparency & Visibility
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Safeguards block, module 15.*

A power you cannot see is a power you cannot check. Every safeguard in this domain — the firewall, the oversight, the renewal test — assumes someone can actually observe what an economic actor is doing, and none of them work in the dark. Visibility is one of the Stack's four load-bearing properties (README), and this module is where the economy is required to provide it.

**Core rule — economic operation at scale must be observable from outside.** Markets must be legible in their prices and terms (ECON-03); money's movement at systemic scale must be auditable (ECON-02); systemic actors must show their real position (ECON-14). The common thread: the larger the economic power, the more it must be seeable by those it affects and those who oversee it. Opacity at scale is not privacy — it is the cover under which capture and extraction operate unseen until they are too large to stop.

**Visibility scales with power, inversely to the person.** This is the ownership rule (ECON-07) applied to disclosure, and the contrast is the whole point: a household's finances are private; a systemic firm's are not. As an actor's economic weight rises, its claim to operate unseen falls, because the harm its hidden conduct can do rises with it. Small and personal earns privacy; large and systemic earns daylight. The framework does not trade away individual privacy to watch power — it distinguishes the two.

**This is about power, not people.** The line above matters enough to state twice. Transparency here targets the operations of economic *power* — the firm, the market, the systemic position — not the records of individual people. What may be collected, retained, and seen about *persons* is a different question with a different answer, and it belongs to **DATA**, not ECON. A transparency regime that became a surveillance regime would have failed the Stack's rule that information stay limited in what it keeps; keeping that boundary clean is part of getting visibility right.

**Visibility is a precondition, not a report.** Disclosure that arrives too late or too vague to act on is not visibility. The standard is whether an outside party — an overseer, a counterparty, the public — can actually see enough, in time, to check the power in question. The measurable forms of that sufficiency are carried by the standards the other safeguards reference (ECON-A1, ECON-A2); this module sets the requirement they have to meet.

*Wager named: that economic power at scale owes visibility to those it affects, and that opacity is a privilege power does not get to keep. The opposing view — that commercial confidentiality is itself valuable and that mandated transparency exposes legitimate advantage and burdens enterprise — is coherent and widely held; this framework does not take it, on the grounds that unseen power cannot be bounded.*

## ECON-16-Interruption-and-Dissolution

*Source: `ECON-16-Interruption-and-Dissolution.md`*

# ECON-16 — Interruption & Dissolution
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Safeguards block, module 16.*

Renewal handles the ordinary case: an authority's term ends, and it must earn another (ECON-06). This module handles the other case — when something has gone wrong enough that you cannot wait for the term to lapse. It is the economy's stop button: the power to halt or dissolve an economic actor *for cause*, mid-term, when the harm is real and waiting is not an option.

**Core rule — every systemic actor must be stoppable and dissolvable without collapsing the system around it.** Stoppability is one of the Stack's four load-bearing properties (README), and an economy honors it only if its largest actors can actually be halted or unwound. The requirement has a hard corollary: an actor that *cannot* be stopped without catastrophe is, for that reason alone, already too entangled — the "too big to fail" condition that ECON-14 forbids. Resolvability is not a feature added at the end; it is a condition of being allowed to operate at scale.

**Interruption is distinct from lapse, and kept distinct on purpose.** Lapse (ECON-06) is neutral, scheduled, and proves nothing against anyone — a term simply ending. Interruption is active and for-cause: a finding that an actor is causing harm it cannot repair, breaching the floor (ECON-12), or operating past the firewall (ECON-13), and must be stopped now. Keeping the two paths separate is what stops the emergency power from being used as a routine one — for-cause removal must clear a real bar, precisely because it is the sharper tool.

**A stop must be reversible where possible, and bounded always.** The power to halt is itself a power, and it sits under the same limits as the rest of the domain. Interruption is constrained by due process, by transparency (ECON-15), and by the principle that it should disrupt no more than the harm requires. An economy that could dissolve any actor at will, without check, has simply relocated the unaccountable power rather than removed it.

**The authority to pull it is GOV's.** ECON defines that systemic actors must be stoppable and dissolvable, and what conditions trigger a for-cause stop. *Who* holds and exercises that authority — the bodies, the process, the appeal — is GOV's to define. ECON builds the off-switch into the design; GOV decides whose hand is on it.

*Wager named: that systemic actors must be built to be stopped and unwound for cause, even where that resolvability costs efficiency and integration. The opposing view — that forced interruption is destabilizing, that the threat of it chills enterprise, and that markets resolve failures better than authorities do — is coherent and widely held; this framework does not take it, on the grounds that a power that cannot be stopped cannot be bounded.*

## ECON-17-Trade-Between-Communities

*Source: `ECON-17-Trade-Between-Communities.md`*

# ECON-17 — Trade Between Communities
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · External block, module 17.*

Everything so far governs an economy looking inward. This module turns it outward — to trade between one community and another, across the border where one economy's rules stop and another's begin. ECON-09 named the principle in passing; this is its detail. The border changes the parties, the distance, and the leverage. It does not change the rule.

**Core rule — the harm-first limit does not stop at the border.** Trade between communities may not become a channel for doing across a line what would be forbidden within it: exporting harm, dumping costs onto another's commons or future, or capturing another community's essentials (ECON-09). An economy that keeps its own house bounded while using trade to extract from its neighbors has not honored the framework — it has merely moved the extraction offshore. Harm-first travels with the goods.

**Trade is coordination between communities, judged the same way.** The test for cross-border trade is the test for all exchange (ECON-01): legitimate to the degree it provisions and coordinates between the parties, suspect to the degree it merely extracts or captures. Trade that lets two communities provision each other better than either could alone is the honest case. Trade built to corner a weaker partner's necessities, flood it with offloaded costs, or lock it into dependence is the inversion — and the asymmetry of power between communities is exactly where that inversion hides.

**Essentials and ecology bind hardest across borders.** The two limits the domain holds most absolutely do not loosen with distance. A community may not be priced out of its own essentials by an outside actor (ECON-04, ECON-12), and ecological harm exported across a border is still ecological harm — the hard limits of ECON-05 are not escaped by relocating the damage to where the buyer cannot see it. Distance is not a discount on the floor or the wall.

**Sovereignty and the long horizon sit elsewhere.** ECON defines the economic conduct of cross-border trade. The *authority* to make treaties, set terms, and bind a community to them is GOV's; the *intergenerational and ecological stewardship* of trade relationships is held with LT. ECON's claim is narrow and firm: whatever the treaty says, trade may not be used to do harm that the framework forbids at home.

*Wager named: that trade between communities is bound by the same harm-first and anti-capture limits as trade within one — that you may not export what you may not do. The opposing view — that cross-border trade is best governed by comparative advantage and mutual gain, and that imposing internal limits on it forgoes prosperity for all parties — is coherent and widely held; this framework does not take it, on the grounds that harm offshored is harm still.*

## ECON-A1-Concentration-Thresholds

*Source: `ECON-A1-Concentration-Thresholds.md`*

# ECON-A1 — Concentration Thresholds
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Appendix & Reference block, module A1.*

Status: Standard (provisional values)

## Purpose

Defines the measurable lines that the anti-capture firewall (ECON-13), systemic oversight (ECON-14), ownership limits (ECON-07), and the renewal test (ECON-06) enforce against. The core modules set the principle — concentration is capped before abuse; this standard sets what "too concentrated" means in measurable terms.

Structure is canonical. The specific numeric values are provisional and set per domain or sector by the overseeing authority (GOV); this document defines what must be measured and how the tiers work, not the final figures.

## I. What is measured

Concentration is assessed across four dimensions, not market share alone:

1. **Market share** — the actor's portion of a defined market.
2. **Systemic weight** — the harm that would follow the actor's failure or withdrawal.
3. **Chokepoint control** — control of an input, channel, or platform others must pass through.
4. **Cross-holding and influence** — control exercised through ownership stakes, board interlocks, and dependency rather than direct share.

A low score on one dimension does not clear a high score on another. Chokepoint control is capturing even at modest market share.

## II. Tiers

- **Tier 1 — Unrestricted.** Below all thresholds. Ordinary operation; no heightened obligation.
- **Tier 2 — Monitored.** Approaching a threshold on any dimension. Triggers disclosure (ECON-15) and review.
- **Tier 3 — Capped.** At a threshold. Further growth on that dimension is barred; structural remedies (divestiture, separation) available.
- **Essential override.** For essentials (ECON-04), the tier boundaries are set tighter, and Tier 3 may bar concentration that would clear elsewhere.

## III. Failure conditions

- Cumulative concentration assembled through many sub-threshold steps (roll-ups, serial acquisition).
- Reclassifying or redefining a market to appear below threshold.
- Concentration via cross-holdings and influence not captured by share alone.
- Chokepoint control treated as acceptable because headline market share is low.

## IV. Review

Thresholds are reviewed and renewed (ECON-A5) on a defined cycle. Values that no longer reflect real concentration risk are revised; the standard itself is not exempt from renewal.

# ECON-A2 — Monetary Issuance & Stability Standard
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Appendix & Reference block, module A2.*

Status: Standard (provisional values)

## Purpose

Defines the measurable issuance and stability requirements behind money's behavioral rules (ECON-02). ECON-02 sets the principle — money stays a stable, legible, bounded means of coordination; this standard sets what stability and legibility require in measurable terms.

This standard governs money's *behavior*. The authority to issue and back currency is GOV's (GOV-14; GOV-A3, Monetary Issuance Rules). Where they meet: GOV holds issuance authority; this standard defines the stability and visibility conditions the issued money must satisfy.

## I. Stability requirements

1. **Bounded variation.** Purchasing power held within a defined stability band over defined periods. Severe inflation and deflation are breaches, not outcomes (ECON-02).
2. **Predictable issuance.** Money created against a rule, not at discretion in the moment; changes to the rule are visible in advance.
3. **No silent debasement.** Any expansion of the money supply is recorded and observable; debasement hidden from holders is a transparency breach (ECON-15).

## II. Visibility requirements

- Systemic-scale monetary movement is auditable (ECON-15).
- Issuance, reserves, and backing are disclosed sufficiently for outside verification.
- The stability band and issuance rule are public, not internal.

## III. Failure conditions

- Variation outside the band falling hardest on those with the least buffer (the harm ECON-02 names).
- Discretionary issuance used to transfer value without provision (extraction by debasement).
- Backing or reserves opaque to outside audit.
- Stability sacrificed to fund a privileged actor or position.

## IV. Review

The stability band, issuance rule, and disclosure requirements are reviewed and renewed (ECON-A5) on a defined cycle, in coordination with the issuing authority (GOV). Provisional values are revised on evidence; the standard's structure is not exempt from renewal.

# ECON-A3 — Provision Floor Standard
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Appendix & Reference block, module A3.*

Status: Standard (provisional values)

## Purpose

Defines what counts as adequate access to each essential — the measurable content of the provision floor (ECON-12) and the essentials rule (ECON-04). The core modules establish that a guaranteed floor exists and is unconditional; this standard sets what the floor must actually deliver, so a guarantee cannot be satisfied in name while failing in fact.

Structure is canonical. The specific adequacy levels are provisional and set to context (geography, climate, cost) by the overseeing authority; this document defines what must be guaranteed and how adequacy is judged, not the final figures.

## I. Covered essentials

The floor covers the finite, public, harm-defined list of ECON-04: water, food, shelter, warmth, basic health care, sanitation, and the ecological life-support these rest on (ECON-05). The list is held narrow on purpose (ECON-04); additions require explicit justification on harm grounds, not convenience.

## II. Adequacy test

For each essential, the floor must define and meet:

1. **Sufficiency** — enough to prevent the harm that absence causes, not a token amount.
2. **Reliability** — available continuously, not intermittently or contingent on application cycles.
3. **Reach** — actually reaching everyone, including those hardest to serve; coverage gaps are failures, not edge cases.
4. **Unconditionality** — delivered without conduct conditions, means-test leverage, or work requirements (ECON-12).
5. **Dignity** — provided without process so burdensome or degrading that it functions as a deterrent to access.

## III. Failure conditions

- A floor defined so low it does not prevent the harm (a floor in name only).
- Access gated behind conditions, compliance, or barriers that make the guarantee revocable in practice.
- Coverage that reaches most but strands the worst-off — the people the floor exists for.
- Funding left discretionary, so the floor fails in lean years (ECON-11, ECON-12).

## IV. Review

Adequacy levels are reviewed and renewed (ECON-A5) on a defined cycle and adjusted to real conditions. The floor's content is revisable; its unconditional character is not.

## ECON-A4-Ecological-and-Externality-Accounting-Standard

*Source: `ECON-A4-Ecological-and-Externality-Accounting-Standard.md`*

# ECON-A4 — Ecological & Externality Accounting Standard
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Appendix & Reference block, module A4.*

Status: Standard (provisional values)

## Purpose

Defines how ecological costs are identified, measured, and internalized — the measurable machinery behind ecological bounds (ECON-05) and full-cost pricing (ECON-09). The core modules establish two tiers: hard limits that no price may cross, and accounted costs that activity must carry. This standard sets how the line between them is drawn and how the accounted tier is measured.

Structure is canonical. The specific thresholds and prices are provisional and set per ecosystem and context by the overseeing authority, in coordination with LT and GOV-18; this document defines the method, not the final figures.

## I. The two tiers (from ECON-05)

- **Hard limits — not priced.** Irreversible or catastrophic thresholds: species extinction, climate tipping points, destruction of an aquifer or living soil, accumulation of persistent toxins. These are ceilings, not costs. No price clears them. This standard's job at the hard tier is to *identify and monitor* the thresholds, not to price them.
- **Accounted costs — measured and internalized.** Reversible, sub-threshold costs. Measured, attributed to the activity that creates them, and carried by it (ECON-09), so the price a buyer sees includes the cost a stranger would otherwise pay.

## II. Accounting requirements (accounted tier)

1. **Identification** — the ecological costs of an activity are named, not omitted.
2. **Attribution** — costs are assigned to the activity and actor that create them.
3. **Full-cost pricing** — the carried cost is reflected in price (ECON-09); a price missing its ecological cost is treated as incomplete, not low.
4. **No threshold laundering** — accounted-tier pricing may never be used to purchase passage past a hard limit (ECON-05). Pricing is a tool beneath the wall, never a key through it.

## III. Failure conditions

- Treating a hard-limit harm as an accounted cost to be priced and crossed.
- Costs omitted, understated, or attributed to the commons or the future.
- Internalization on paper that does not reach the price a buyer actually faces.
- Thresholds monitored too late or too coarsely to prevent the irreversible breach.

## IV. Review

Thresholds and accounting methods are reviewed and renewed (ECON-A5) on a defined cycle, with LT holding the long-horizon view. The hard-limit list expands as evidence of irreversibility accrues; it does not contract for convenience.

# ECON-A5 — Structural Renewal Review Protocol
*Civilization Stack · ECON domain · Appendix & Reference block, module A5.*

Status: Standard (provisional values)

## Purpose

Defines the procedure by which standing economic authority is reviewed for renewal — the machinery behind renewal logic (ECON-06). ECON-06 establishes that economic authority defaults to expiration and must earn continuation; this protocol sets what an actor must demonstrate, how the review runs, and what happens when evidence is absent.

The protocol mirrors the Stack's wider renewal-evidence approach: continuation requires affirmative demonstration, and absence of evidence defaults to non-renewal.

## I. What is subject to review

Per ECON-06: charters and operating authority of large or systemic firms; any monopoly or near-monopoly position, even a permitted one; special privileges (subsidies, exclusive licenses, exemptions, guarantees); and the mandates of the oversight bodies themselves. Nothing is exempt, including this protocol.

## II. Renewal evidence (the test from ECON-06)

To renew, an authority must demonstrate, with documented evidence, that it:

1. **Still serves provision and coordination** (ECON-01) — not merely persists.
2. **Sits inside the harm and ecological limits** (ECON-00, ECON-05, ECON-A4).
3. **Has not concentrated past the thresholds** (ECON-13, ECON-A1).
4. **Remains stoppable, reversible, and visible** (ECON-15, ECON-16).

Absence of documented evidence in any one of these defaults to non-renewal. The burden is on the authority to prove *for* continuation, never on others to prove *against* it.

## III. Lapse versus for-cause removal

- **Lapse** — a term ending for failed or absent renewal. Neutral, scheduled, no finding of wrongdoing (ECON-06). The ordinary default.
- **For-cause removal** — active interruption or dissolution mid-term on a finding of harm (ECON-16). A separate, sharper path with a higher bar.

This protocol governs lapse. It hands the for-cause path to ECON-16, and the authority to act in either case to GOV.

## IV. Failure conditions

- Renewal granted by inertia, without affirmative evidence ("it would be disruptive to stop").
- Burden inverted — requiring proof against an authority rather than proof from it.
- Oversight bodies exempting their own mandates from review.
- Cumulative scope or concentration expansion passed over because each step looked minor.

## V. Review

The protocol is itself reviewed and renewed on a defined cycle. A renewal procedure that exempted itself from renewal would fail the first test it sets (ECON-06).

## ECON-MASTER

*Source: `ECON-MASTER.md`*

# 1. Purpose

The Economy domain defines the operational systems governing production, exchange, allocation, labor, ownership, pricing, and resource sustainability across civilization-scale systems.

Its purpose is not to study economics as an academic discipline, but to define the actual economic architecture required for long-term civilizational continuity within the constraints established by BPF.

The domain establishes:

- what economic systems are for
- how incentives remain aligned with sustainability
- how resources are allocated
- how ecological constraints are enforced
- how ownership and labor function
- how exchange systems avoid destabilizing concentration and extraction dynamics

The Economy domain exists to ensure that civilization can sustain itself materially without producing runaway instability, collapse incentives, or ecological destruction.

---

# 5. Structural Principles

Every ECON module operates under these principles:

- Harm is prevented and repaired before activity proceeds.
- Economic authority does not continue automatically; it is renewed on evidence.
- Institutions stay interruptible, reversible, and auditable.
- Access to essentials is a floor, not a market outcome.
- Nothing becomes too big to fail, stop, or leave.
- Natural limits are hard constraints, not externalities.
- Money serves coordination, not extraction.

# Economy

Status: Framework (core complete)

## Purpose

Defines the operational resource, incentive, exchange, production, and sustainability systems required for civilization-scale coordination and continuity.

The Economy domain is not primarily concerned with economic theory as an academic discipline. It defines the actual economic system architecture required to sustain stable civilization over long time horizons.

## Scope

- Resource allocation
- Currency and exchange systems
- Markets and competition
- Ownership structures
- Labor and income systems
- Ecological constraints
- Renewal and regeneration logic
- Credit, taxation, and the provision floor
- Anti-capture, oversight, transparency, and interruption safeguards
- Cross-community trade
- Trade and pricing mechanisms
- Economic stability boundaries

## Failure Mode

Economic failure produces extractive behavior, instability, unsustainable growth pressures, concentration collapse, ecological overshoot, and breakdown of long-term coordination capacity.

## Relationship to BPF

BPF defines the governing principles, limits, and civilizational boundary conditions.

The Economy domain operationalizes sustainable production, exchange, and allocation systems within those constraints.
